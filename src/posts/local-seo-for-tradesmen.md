For trades involving safety, urgency, or access to someone's home, that online search is where the trust decision gets made — often before anyone picks up the phone.

## What is local SEO, and how is it different from regular SEO?

Regular SEO aims for national or international search visibility — ranking content regardless of where the searcher is located. Local SEO targets geographic results: your business appearing when someone in your service area searches for your trade. For tradesmen, local SEO is almost always the correct priority. You are not competing with the whole of the UK; you are competing with the handful of plumbers or electricians within a ten-mile radius.

Google's local search algorithm ranks businesses on three factors: **proximity** (how close you are to the searcher), **relevance** (how closely your content matches what they searched), and **prominence** (how established and trusted your business appears, measured by reviews, citations, and website authority). Local SEO optimises all three simultaneously.

The most visible outcome is appearance in the **Google Local Pack** — the map and three business listings that appear above the organic results for searches like "plumber near me" or "heating engineer Cambridge." A 2024 BrightLocal study found that the Local Pack receives 42% of all clicks on a local search results page. If you are not appearing there, the majority of actively searching customers never see you.

One practical advantage local SEO has over national SEO: the competition is real but beatable. A Cambridge plumber does not need to outrank the whole internet — only the other plumbers in Cambridgeshire. With the right foundations in place, that is an achievable goal within a few months of consistent work.

## Your Google Business Profile is the foundation — get this right first

Your Google Business Profile (GBP) is the single most important asset for local SEO as a tradesperson. It is what generates your appearance in the Local Pack, and a complete, well-optimised profile consistently outperforms a partial one. Google's own research has found that businesses with complete profiles receive seven times more clicks than those with incomplete ones.

The non-negotiables for a trades GBP:

**Business name and category.** Your primary category should be as specific as possible — "Plumber," "Electrician," "Roofer" — not a catch-all like "Contractor." Your business name must match your trading name exactly, and that name must be identical across your website and every directory listing. Character-for-character consistency matters more than most people realise.

**Service area, precisely defined.** Add every town and postcode district you genuinely serve. Do not pad this out — Google cross-references your declared service area against where your reviews, website content, and activity suggest you actually operate. Overstating your area can suppress your rankings in the places you actually work.

**Services section, fully populated.** Add individual services with descriptions. "Consumer unit replacement," "EV charger installation," "emergency call-out" — each service is an opportunity to match a query someone in your area types into Google. Most tradespeople leave this section blank or fill it with three lines. That is a straightforward ranking opportunity left on the table.

**Photos: real ones, updated regularly.** Google's own research shows that GBP profiles with photos receive 42% more requests for directions and 35% more website clicks than profiles without. Upload photos of your completed work, your van, your certificates, and ideally your face. Phone camera quality is fine. Authenticity matters more than production value for a trades business.

**Posts, used consistently.** GBP posts function like short updates — a recent job, a seasonal offer, a note about your availability. They signal to Google that your business is active, which supports local ranking stability over time.

I have seen tradespeople with genuinely impressive operations sitting outside the Local Pack because their GBP was half-finished. Business name inconsistency, no service area set, four photos from four years ago. Fixing those things moved the needle within weeks. It is the highest-return action most tradespeople can take right now, and it costs nothing except time.

## What does on-page local SEO look like for a trades website?

On-page local SEO means structuring your website so Google can clearly identify what you do, where you do it, and who you serve. According to Moz's Local Search Ranking Factors research, on-page signals — the content, structure, and technical setup of your website — remain among the most influential factors in local ranking, alongside your GBP. Most trades websites get this wrong by being too vague, too thin, or built for a general audience rather than a local one.

The practical components:

**One service page per service.** "Boiler installation Cambridge" is a page. "Emergency plumber Ely" is a page. A single page listing every service you have ever provided is a missed ranking opportunity for every one of those search terms. Each dedicated page should answer: what is the service, where do you offer it, what does it involve, and how does the customer contact you?

**Your location, named throughout — naturally.** Your target town or region should appear in your page title, your opening paragraph, and throughout the page in context. Not repeated mechanically — used as a real writer would use it, because it is genuinely relevant. "We install and service boilers across Cambridge, Ely, and St Ives" is both useful information for a customer and a meaningful signal for Google.

**Schema markup.** This is structured data invisible to visitors but tells Google exactly who you are, where you operate, your hours, and what you do. `LocalBusiness` schema and trade-specific types such as `Plumber` or `ElectricalContractor` are particularly relevant here. Google's own Search Central documentation confirms that structured data helps it understand your content more accurately. The vast majority of trades websites have none of it. That is an open goal.

**Consistent NAP data.** Your Name, Address, and Phone number must be identical across your website, your GBP, and every directory listing — character for character. Inconsistencies are one of the most common causes of suppressed local rankings I find when auditing trade websites. Simple to fix; surprisingly easy to overlook.

## Do local citations still matter for tradesmen?

Local citations — mentions of your business name, address, and phone number on directories and third-party sites — remain a meaningful local ranking signal in 2026. Moz's Local Search Ranking Factors research consistently identifies citation signals among the top contributors to Local Pack rankings. Their influence relative to GBP and on-page signals has declined somewhat over the past few years, but accurate citations across authoritative directories continue to support local rankings in competitive markets.

For tradesmen, the most valuable citations come from:

- **Checkatrade, TrustATrader, and MyBuilder** — even if you no longer actively use them for leads, a complete and accurate listing on each supports your local authority
- **Yell.com** and **Thomson Local** — legacy directories that retain genuine domain authority
- **Trade body and certification websites** — Gas Safe Register, NICEIC, NAPIT listings carry both trust signals and citation value; these are particularly strong because Google understands their relevance to your trade
- **Your local council's business directory** — often overlooked, reliably useful
- **Bing Places for Business** — frequently neglected, but Bing accounts for approximately 6% of UK search volume and feeds Apple Maps

What damages rankings more than absent citations is inconsistent ones. If your GBP says "Martin's Heating Ltd," your Yell listing says "Martins Heating," and your website footer says "Martin Spooner Heating & Plumbing," Google is looking at three different businesses. Clean up inconsistencies before building new citations.

I have audited trades websites where excellent on-page SEO and a strong GBP were being undermined by a decade of accumulated directory listings with three different phone numbers and two different trading names. Citation tidying is unglamorous work. It moves the needle reliably.

## How long does local SEO take to show results for a tradesperson?

Local SEO for tradesmen typically produces visible ranking improvement within two to four months for lower-competition searches, and three to six months for more competitive terms in larger towns or cities. This aligns with Google's own published guidance, which acknowledges that significant site changes can take "several months" to be reflected in search results. The timeline depends on your starting point, the competition in your area, and how thoroughly the foundations are put in place.

I will be straightforward about this, because it is a question that attracts a lot of misleading answers. "Results in weeks" is occasionally true for GBP changes; it is almost never true for competitive organic rankings. Local SEO is not advertising — it does not switch on the day you launch. What it does is compound. A well-optimised website with consistent citations and a growing review base builds ranking positions that hold without ongoing ad spend.

The fastest results typically come from:

1. **Google Business Profile optimisation** — changes here can affect Local Pack visibility within days to a few weeks
2. **Fixing NAP inconsistencies** — resolving citation conflicts can show ranking improvement within a month
3. **Adding service and location pages** — new pages can begin ranking within six to ten weeks of being indexed by Google
4. **Gathering reviews consistently** — each new review adds a fresh signal and can directly affect Local Pack position

The slowest component is domain authority — the overall trust Google places in your website, built over months and years through content, links, and consistent online presence. It cannot be rushed. But doing everything else correctly accelerates it.

## Frequently asked questions

**What is the difference between local SEO and paying for leads on Checkatrade?**

Paying for lead directories is a recurring cost that stops generating work the moment you stop paying. Local SEO builds an owned asset — your Google presence — that continues generating enquiries without a per-lead fee. Most tradespeople who invest properly in local SEO reduce or eliminate their directory spend within 12 to 18 months. The upfront investment is higher; the long-term cost per enquiry is far lower. You also own the relationship with the customer from the first contact, rather than being one of several quoted options on a directory page.

**How many Google reviews do I need to rank in the Local Pack?**

There is no fixed threshold. BrightLocal's research consistently finds that businesses in the top three Local Pack positions have significantly more reviews than competitors appearing lower. More important than total volume is recency — a steady stream of new reviews over time outperforms a large burst of old ones. For most UK trades markets outside major city centres, ten to twenty genuinely recent reviews is a strong starting position. The operative word is "genuinely": authentic reviews from real customers, not solicited in ways that violate Google's review policies.

**Do I need a separate website page for every town I work in?**

Yes, if you want to rank for searches in those towns. A location page needs to be genuinely useful — describing your services in that area, including local context if relevant, and containing your contact details with the area clearly named. A thin template with the town name swapped in will not rank and may actively harm your credibility with Google's helpful content systems. If you cover eight towns, build eight proper pages. It is more work; it produces meaningfully better results.

**Can I do local SEO myself as a tradesperson?**

The fundamentals are absolutely achievable without specialist help: claiming and fully optimising your GBP, ensuring your website clearly names your services and locations, and gathering reviews consistently. Where most tradespeople run short on time or technical knowledge is schema markup, citation management across dozens of directories, and diagnosing why rankings are stalling. Starting with the GBP is free and immediately impactful — it is the right first step for anyone doing this themselves.

**What happens to my local rankings if I change my phone number or move premises?**

Update everything simultaneously and immediately: your GBP, your website, and every major directory listing. If your website URL also changes, submit a redirect and notify Google via Search Console. Stale NAP data from a previous address or phone number can suppress rankings for months if it persists across directories. I have seen tradespeople lose their Local Pack position for the better part of a year after a premises move that was never properly communicated across their online presence. The update itself takes a few hours; the recovery from neglecting it can take much longer.

**Does having a professionally built website actually make a difference to local SEO?**

Yes, substantially — but not because of aesthetics. A professionally built trades website includes the technical architecture that makes local SEO work: proper schema markup, separate service and location pages, mobile-first performance, clean NAP data in the footer, and a site structure Google can index efficiently. A DIY template on Wix or Squarespace can rank to a point, but tends to hit a ceiling on technical SEO that a properly built site does not. If you are serious about winning work from local search in a competitive area, the website is the infrastructure the rest of your local SEO sits on.

**Should I run Google Ads alongside local SEO, or choose one or the other?**

They serve different timelines. Google Ads can generate enquiries from day one — useful while your organic rankings are building. Local SEO builds a position that does not depend on a monthly budget. For most tradespeople, the most cost-effective approach is to run modest, tightly targeted ads in the early months, then reduce ad spend as organic and Local Pack rankings develop.
